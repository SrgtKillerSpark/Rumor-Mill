# Rumor Mill — Narrative Identity

*The creative reference document for tone, world, characters, and story. All copy, scenario writing, and creative decisions should align with this document.*

---

## Premise

You are a hired agent in a medieval town. You carry no sword, hold no title, and own no land. Your only currency is information — specifically, the gap between what people *know* and what they *believe*.

Thirty people live in this town. They wake, work, worship, drink, and gossip. They protect their friends and distrust strangers. They repeat what they hear, and they add to it. By the time a story reaches its tenth pair of ears, it barely resembles the truth — but it carries all the weight of it.

Your employer has a target. Your job is to move a reputation: up, down, or out the door. You have whispers, timing, and a careful read of who talks to whom. Everything else is up to the town.

---

## Tone & Voice

**Medieval intrigue, not high fantasy.** No magic, no chosen heroes, no prophecy. The power here is entirely social. A rumor is not a spell — but it behaves like one.

**Dark humor runs underneath everything.** The player is doing something morally questionable for coin, in service of patrons whose motives are rarely pure. The game doesn't pretend otherwise. Victory narrations are dry and unsentimental. The work was done. The town chose its own story.

**The NPCs are not enemies.** They are credulous, loyal, and human. They protect people they love and repeat things they shouldn't. When a campaign works, it's because the player understood those humans better than they understood themselves — not because they outsmarted a puzzle.

**Language style:** Understated, period-adjacent. Avoid anachronism. Avoid over-dramatization. Write dialogue and narration as if it were recorded by a neutral clerk with a faintly sardonic worldview.

---

## Key Themes

**1. Truth is a resource, not a constraint.**
The most effective rumors are not lies — they are carefully true-adjacent stories shaped for maximum believability and minimum resistance. The player who understands this wins faster.

**2. Loyalty is a weapon that cuts both ways.**
Tight faction bonds protect targets from rumor campaigns. They also become vectors when a trusted ally is compromised. Understanding who is loyal to whom — and what that loyalty costs — is the core strategic skill.

**3. Information decays.**
Every rumor has a half-life. NPCs forget, recover, and defend. Heat builds, making the next intervention harder. The player who moves slowly enough to be precise but fast enough to finish before the town reconstitutes is the winner.

**4. The spymaster's moral ambiguity.**
Scenarios 1 and 2 implicate the player in genuinely damaging acts — discrediting a person, driving out a stranger. Scenario 4 inverts this by casting the player as protector. There is no clean version of this work. The game holds that tension without resolving it.

**5. Emergence over authorship.**
The player plants seeds. The town grows them into something else. The pleasure is in watching a carefully constructed lie take root, mutate, and become a social fact — independent of its origin.

## World-Building Pillars

**A walled town, not a kingdom.** The world is exactly as large as the map. The walls are implied. Other cities, other powers, other patrons exist — but they never appear. This compression makes every social relationship feel consequential.

**Economy of the everyday.** The drama unfolds in a tavern, a market stall, a chapel, a guard post. The settings are mundane and the stakes are personal: a tax roll, a succession, an accusation. Medieval politics at the scale where most people actually lived them.

**Factions as culture, not teams.** Merchants talk fastest. Nobles distrust first. Clergy have the deepest loyalty and the highest moral authority to contradict a story publicly. These are not mechanical resistances — they are worldviews expressed as behavior.

**The day as structure.** A tick is one twenty-fourth of a day. NPCs have schedules. The tavern fills at dusk. The market empties at noon. The chapel bell calls people together. Time pressure is not abstracted — it is the 30 days until the autumn tax rolls are signed, the 20 days before the inquisitor finishes his interviews. The clock is always in the room.

**History outside the frame.** Edric Fenn has run this town for nine years. Lady Isolde has been patient for longer. Brother Cornelius has done this in other towns. The player arrives into a world already in motion, does their work, and leaves. The world continues without them.

## Scenarios

### 1 — The Alderman's Ruin
*Tutorial. Duration: 30 days. No bribery.*

A patron has sent you to discredit Lord Edric Fenn, the corrupt alderman who has held the town for nine years through loyalty and fear. The autumn tax assessment arrives in twelve days. If he's still in office then, the patron's candidate loses another term.

Edric is nearly immune to direct persuasion (credulity: 0.05). He must be brought down through faction sentiment — eroding the trust the town has placed in him, not the trust he has in himself. Eavesdrop detection causes instant failure; this scenario rewards patience over speed.

**Victory:** A petition with seven signatures arrives at dawn. Edric steps down by midday, looking ill. You are already three days' ride away.

**Failure:** The tax rolls are signed. The window closed.

---

### 2 — The Plague Scare
*Duration: 24 days. Bribery enabled (2 charges).*

A rival herbalist named Alys has arrived with the autumn rains, undercutting the midwife who employs you. Illness rumors travel fast. Your job is to plant one precisely enough to drive Alys out of town before Sister Maren — the most morally credible NPC in the network — publicly contradicts it.

If Maren rejects the illness rumor, the campaign collapses. Seven NPCs believing or actively spreading the story is the win threshold. This scenario teaches the cost of over-seeding.

**Victory:** Alys packs her cart before dawn. Three days later, Maren declares the illness talk was unfounded — but by then Alys is gone.

**Failure:** Maren stands in Market Square and names every NPC who spread the rumor. The campaign is exposed.

---

### 3 — The Succession
*Duration: 25 days. Bribery enabled (2 charges). AI opponent active.*

Lady Isolde Fenn wants to elevate her son Calder to power. Tomas Reeve, a tax collector with half the Merchant Quarter behind him, blocks the path. You must raise Calder's reputation to 75 and bring Tomas below 35 — while a rival agent actively plants counter-rumors every few days, escalating in intensity over three phases.

If Calder's reputation drops below 35, he becomes the subject of the scandal rather than its beneficiary. This scenario demands simultaneous offense, defense, and anticipation of an intelligent opponent.

**Victory:** Winter festival nominations are read. Calder's name draws a real cheer. Tomas's draws silence. Lady Isolde watches from her window without smiling — but exhales.

**Failure:** Both names draw polite applause. Lady Isolde's expression in the corridor is unambiguous.

---

### 4 — The Holy Inquisition
*Duration: 20 days. No bribery. Purely defensive.*

Brother Cornelius, an inquisitor, arrives with the Bishop's seal and three names: Aldous Prior, Vera Midwife, and Finn Monk — accused of heresy, improper doctrine, and unsanctioned healing. You owe each of them a debt that cannot be repaid in coin. Twenty days to keep their reputations at or above 48.

This scenario inverts the game's usual role. You are not the aggressor but the defender, countering the inquisitor's propaganda with positive rumors while managing the wildly different personality profiles of your three protectees (the Prior is nearly unshakable; the Monk is dangerously credulous and forgetful).

**Victory:** Brother Cornelius leaves at dawn on Day 21, writ unsigned. The Bishop's clerk records "insufficient evidence." In reality, plenty existed — it simply wasn't believed. The three resume their lives. The town chose its own.

**Failure:** The crowd gathers at chapel steps. They come with stories. By evening, the writ is signed.

## Character Archetypes

### The Gatekeeper
*Examples: Edric Fenn (Noble), Aldous Prior (Clergy), Aldric Vane (Merchant)*

High loyalty, low credulity. Resistant to rumors. When they reject a story, they enter DEFENDING state — actively suppressing the rumor's spread among their neighbors. These characters are walls. You do not target them directly; you work around them, isolating them from their allies.

### The Conduit
*Examples: Nell Picker (Merchant), Greta Flint (Merchant), Constance Widow (Clergy)*

High sociability, high credulity. The town's most effective gossip vectors. A story planted through a Conduit reaches the widest network fastest — but it also mutates most often. Use them for speed at the cost of narrative control.

### The Anchor
*Examples: Sister Maren (Clergy), Bram Guard (Noble)*

High loyalty to a specific subject or faction. When they reject a negative rumor about someone they trust, they enter DEFENDING state and can collapse an entire neighborhood's credulity for that story. Identifying Anchors before seeding is critical.

### The Vulnerable
*Examples: Finn Monk (Clergy), Cob Farrow (Merchant), Old Hugh (Noble)*

Low loyalty, high credulity. Believes quickly, forgets quickly, has few protective relationships. Easy to influence, easy to lose. Not reliable vectors for a campaign that needs to hold — but useful for short-term momentum.

### The Firewall
*Examples: Rufus Bolt (Merchant), Bess Wicker (Merchant), Annit Scribe (Noble)*

Low sociability. Rumors stall here. They hear little, spread less, and make poor targets or vectors. Their isolation is both a dead end and a safe harbor — sometimes you plant through them when you need a story to age quietly before it spreads.

### The Target-in-Play
*Examples: Calder Fenn (Noble), Alys Herbwife (Clergy)*

Characters whose reputations are the active objective. Their personality stats define the constraints of the campaign: Calder's high temperament makes him act on beliefs quickly, for good or ill; Alys's low sociability means she has fewer defenders but also fewer organic spreaders on her behalf.

## Faction Culture

### Merchants
The social nervous system of the town. High traffic through taverns and market stalls means rumors propagate fast through this faction. The downside: Merchant rumors mutate frequently, and their stories reach the Clergy and Nobles quickly, which can trigger counter-responses. Best for: speed seeding, wide diffusion. Weakness: loose narrative control.

### Nobility
The institutional center. Low credulity at the top (Edric, Bram, Tomas), moderate-to-high at the margins (Calder, Pell Gate, Old Hugh). Nobility protects its own through inter-faction loyalty, and Noble rejection of a story carries social weight that can slow diffusion elsewhere. Best for: targeted reputation campaigns with controlled spread. Weakness: nearly impossible to move leadership without indirect faction pressure first.

### Clergy
The moral authority and the wildest variance. Leadership (Prior, Maren) has extreme loyalty and skepticism; laity (Finn, Old Piety, Constance) are among the most credulous characters in the game. A story that reaches the Clergy's laity becomes deeply believed — and if it then gets contradicted by clergy leadership, that contradiction carries the full weight of moral authority. Best for: deep belief penetration when handled carefully. Weakness: Maren and the Prior can collapse a campaign with a single public rejection.

## Writing Voice Reference

**Victory lines are terse and factual.** The job is done. The reader should feel the quiet satisfaction of a well-executed plan, not triumph.

> *"A petition with seven signatures arrives at dawn. Edric steps down by midday, looking ill. You are already three days' ride away when word reaches the capital."*

**Failure lines are specific and irreversible.** Name what changed. The window closed, not "you didn't make it."

> *"Sister Maren stands in Market Square and names every NPC who spread the rumor. The campaign is exposed."*

**NPC descriptions are behavioral, not psychological.** Lead with what they do, not who they are.

> *"Nell Picker hears everything that passes through the tavern floor and repeats it with additions. She doesn't lie — she elaborates."*

**The player is never addressed directly in narrative.** Use "you" only in tutorial instruction. In scenario narration, frame all action in third-person or passive: "the rumor reaches," "the town decides," "the writ is signed."
